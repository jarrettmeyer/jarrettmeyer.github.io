## The Exponential Distribution

---

### Exponentional Distribution

Models the time between events. If events arrive at rate $\lambda$ per unit time, the waiting time until the next event is:

$$f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \ge 0$$

The mean waiting time between events is $\frac{1}{\lambda}$.

---

Customers arrive at a coffee shop at an average rate of $\lambda = 3$ customers per hour.

The average time between arrivals is:

$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{3} \space \text{hours} = 20 \space \text{minutes}$$

---

### Probability Density Function (PDF)

$$f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \quad \forall x \geq 0$$

---

---

### Cumulative Density Function (CDF)

$$F(x) = P(X \leq x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x} \quad \forall x \geq 0$$

---

<ExponentialPlot mode="cdf" client:only="react" />

---

### Observations

- $F(0) = 0$. The probability of a 0-length wait is 0.
- CDF approaches 1 as $x$ goes to $\infty$. If you wait long enough, the event is guaranteed to happen.
- Higher $\lambda$ means events happen sooner.

---

### Real-World Examples

- Customer arrivals
- Wait times
- Equipment failure
- Radioactive decay

Key assumptions are **independent** events and **constant average rate**.

## Poisson Processes

---

If the **exponential** distribution tells us the time between events, the **Poisson** distribution tells us the number of events in a given time period.

---

### 1. Partition the Interval

Slice $[0, t]$ into $n$ equal subintervals, each of length $\frac{t}{n}$.

---

### 2. Probability per Subinterval

For large enough $n$, each subinterval is so short that at most one event can happen.

$$P(\text{one event}) \approx \lambda \cdot \frac{t}{n}$$

---

### 3. Each subinterval is a Bernoulli trial

Either an event happens or it doesn't.

$$X \sim \text{Binomial}\!\left(n,\, p\right) = \text{Binomial}\!\left(n,\, \frac{\lambda t}{n}\right)$$

---

### 4. To $\infty$ and Beyond

Let $n \to \infty$...

$$\binom{n}{k} \cdot \frac{1}{n^k} \;\to\; \frac{1}{k!}$$

$$\left(\frac{\lambda t}{n}\right)^k \cdot n^k = (\lambda t)^k$$

$$\left(1 - \frac{\lambda t}{n}\right)^n \;\to\; e^{-\lambda t}$$

Put them all together...

---

### Poisson Distribution!

$$P(X = k) = \frac{(\lambda t)^k \, e^{-\lambda t}}{k!}, \quad k \in \mathbb{N}_0$$

$$P(X = k) = \frac{\mu^k \, e^{-\mu}}{k!}, \quad k \in \mathbb{N}_0$$

---

<PoissonPlot client:only="react" />

---

- Small $\lambda t$: distribution is right-skewed, concentrated near 0
- Large $\lambda t$: distribution spreads, approaches a normal distribution
- Peak is always $k = \lambda t$

---

| Question                       | Distribution                | Type       |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------------- | ---------- |
| How long until the next event? | $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$       | Continuous |
| How many events in time $t$?   | $\text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$ | Discrete   |

The two distributions are mathematically inseparable. If you know one, you know the other.

---

All the same caveats apply. Independent events at a constant rate.
