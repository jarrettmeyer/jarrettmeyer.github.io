Previously, we modeled the time between independent events using the [exponential distribution](/statistics/exponential-distribution). We gave the example of customers arriving at a coffee shop at a rate of $\lambda = 3$ per hour (where the **tick** is one hour). The time between arrivals follows $\text{Exp}(3)$. This gives us an expected gap of 20 minutes between customers.

_Note: If the **tick** were 1 minute, instead of 1 hour, $\lambda = 0.05$. The tick is important!_

Now we flip the question. Instead of asking, "How long until the next customer?", we ask: "How many customers arrive in a given window of time?" If we watch the shop for an entire 8-hour shift, how many customers should we expect?

The expected value is obvious. An average of 3 customers per hour. 8 hours. We should expect 24 customers in 8 hours. But what about every other possibility?

The answer is the **Poisson distribution**.

## From Exponential to Poisson

The derivation starts with the same setup. Events arrive at a constant average rate $\lambda$. We want to count how many events occur in a fixed interval of length $t$.

**Step 1: Partition the interval.** Slice $[0, t]$ into $n$ equal subintervals, each of length $\frac{t}{n}$.

**Step 2: Probability per subinterval.** For large $n$, each subinterval is so short that at most one event can fall in it. The probability of exactly one event in a subinterval of length $\frac{t}{n}$ is approximately:

$$P(\text{one event}) \approx \lambda \cdot \frac{t}{n}$$

**Step 3: Recognize the binomial.** Each subinterval either contains an event or it doesn't, independently of the others. That's a classic binomial setup: $n$ independent trials, each with success probability $p = \frac{\lambda t}{n}$. The total number of events across all $n$ subintervals follows:

$$X \sim \text{Binomial}\!\left(n,\, p\right) = \text{Binomial}\!\left(n,\, \frac{\lambda t}{n}\right)$$

Here $n$ is the number of subintervals (trials) and $p = \frac{\lambda t}{n}$ is the probability of exactly one event in each subinterval. Notice that as $n$ grows, $p$ shrinks proportionally. We continue to slice the interval ever finer while keeping the total expected count $np = \lambda t$ fixed.

The probability of exactly $k$ events is:

$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} \left(\frac{\lambda t}{n}\right)^k \left(1 - \frac{\lambda t}{n}\right)^{n-k}$$

**Step 4: Take the limit.** Let $n \to \infty$ — infinitely many, infinitely thin subintervals. Each factor converges separately:

$$\binom{n}{k} \cdot \frac{1}{n^k} \;\to\; \frac{1}{k!} \qquad \left(\frac{\lambda t}{n}\right)^k \cdot n^k = (\lambda t)^k \qquad \left(1 - \frac{\lambda t}{n}\right)^n \;\to\; e^{-\lambda t}$$

Multiplying the pieces together:

$$P(X = k) = \frac{(\lambda t)^k \, e^{-\lambda t}}{k!}, \quad k \in \mathbb{N}_0$$

This is the **Poisson distribution**.

## The Poisson Distribution

The Poisson **probability mass function** (PMF) has one parameter: $\mu = \lambda t$, the expected number of events in the interval.

**Note:** We say PMF rather than PDF because $k$ is discrete. You can have 0, 1, 2, or 24 customers, but you can't have 3.7 customers. The exponential distribution used a PDF because time is continuous and can take any non-negative real value.

$$P(X = k) = \frac{\mu^k \, e^{-\mu}}{k!}$$

Its key properties:

| Property              | Formula                     |
| --------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Mean (expected value) | $E[X] = \lambda t$          |
| Variance              | $\text{Var}(X) = \lambda t$ |
| Standard deviation    | $\sigma = \sqrt{\lambda t}$ |

The mean and variance are equal. This is the fingerprint of the Poisson distribution — if you fit a Poisson model to real data and find the sample mean and variance are very different, that's a signal the Poisson isn't the right model.

**Back to the coffee shop.** With $\lambda = 3$ and $t = 8$, we get $\mu = \lambda t = 24$. Over a full shift, the expected number of customers is 24. But how does the probability spread?

$$P(X = 20) = \dfrac{24^{20} \, e^{-24}}{20!} \approx 0.0624$$

$$P(X = 24) = \dfrac{24^{24} \, e^{-24}}{24!} \approx 0.0812$$

$$P(X = 30) = \dfrac{24^{30} \, e^{-24}}{30!} \approx 0.0363$$

Even the most likely outcome ($k = 24$) only occurs about 8% of the time. The probability spreads across a wide range. That's typical for a Poisson distribution with a large mean — it looks roughly bell-shaped, which is no accident (the central limit theorem explains why).

## Two Sides of the Same Coin

The exponential distribution and the Poisson distribution describe the same underlying process from two different perspectives. Both stem from the same assumption: events arrive independently at a constant average rate $\lambda$.

| Question                       | Distribution                | Type       |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------------- | ---------- |
| How long until the next event? | $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$       | Continuous |
| How many events in time $t$?   | $\text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$ | Discrete   |

If you know $\lambda$ for one, you know it for the other. The two distributions are mathematically inseparable. They are two ways of viewing the same process.

## Real-World Examples

Anywhere events arrive at a roughly constant, independent rate, you can count them with the Poisson distribution:

- **Customer arrivals.** A coffee shop sees $\lambda = 3$ arrivals per hour. In an 8-hour shift, the customer count follows $\text{Poisson}(24)$.

- **Server requests.** A web server handles $\lambda = 500$ requests per minute. The number of requests in a 10-second window follows $\text{Poisson}(83.3)$.

- **Manufacturing defects.** A factory produces defects at a rate of $\lambda = 0.02$ per item. In a batch of 1,000 items, the defect count follows $\text{Poisson}(20)$.

- **Natural events.** A seismograph records minor tremors at $\lambda = 2$ per day. The weekly count follows $\text{Poisson}(14)$.

The same caveats apply as with the exponential: the Poisson is the right model when events are independent and the rate is roughly constant. If customers tend to arrive in groups, or if the rush-hour rate is three times the off-peak rate, a more nuanced model is needed.
