In our [Poisson process post](/statistics/poisson-process), we derived the distribution from first principles. We sliced a time interval into infinitely thin slices, assigned a probability to each slice, and took the limit as the number of slices approached $\infty$. This simulates the process, so you can watch the number of arrivals grow over time.

## The Simulation Algorithm

We learned from the [exponential distribution](/statistics/exponential-distribution) that if events arrive at rate $\lambda$, the time between consecutive events follows $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$. To simulate a Poisson process, we need to generate exponential interarrival times.

The **inverse-transform method** lets us generate an exponential sample from a uniform random number. Starting with $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$, then:

$$T = -\frac{\ln U}{\lambda}$$

The random variable $T$ follows $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$.

To simulate over an 8-hour window:

1. Draw $U_1 \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$, compute $T_1 = -\ln(U_1) / \lambda$. The first arrival is at time $S_1 = T_1$.
2. Draw $U_2$, compute $T_2$. Second arrival at $S_2 = S_1 + T_2$.
3. Continue accumulating: $S_n = S_{n-1} + T_n$.
4. Stop when $S_n > 8$.

That's the complete algorithm. No approximations, no lookup tables. Uniform random numbers and a logarithm.

## The Counting Function N(t)

The Poisson process is usually written as $N(t)$: the number of arrivals by time $t$. It starts at zero, increases by 1 at each arrival, and is constant between arrivals. The resulting graph is a staircase.

The red dashed line shows the expected value: $E[N(t)] = \lambda t$. This is the deterministic trend that the random staircase fluctuates around. Sometimes the process runs ahead of expectation, sometimes behind. Over many runs, the average staircase converges to that line.

## What to Notice

- **The staircase is a random walk.** It only steps forward — never back — but the underlying mechanism is identical to flipping coins. Every infinitesimal slice of time is a Bernoulli trial; the Poisson distribution is what you get when you take infinitely many of them. Because you're accumulating independent trials, the spread of possible outcomes grows with time, exactly as it does in any random walk. A run that ends far from $\lambda t$ isn't unusual — it's the variance doing what variance does.

- **Higher $\lambda$ means more arrivals and a steeper staircase.** The gaps between steps get shorter. At $\lambda = 12$, you're averaging 96 arrivals over the shift; the staircase looks almost smooth.

- **Lower $\lambda$ makes the gaps visible.** At $\lambda = 0.5$, you might go an hour or two without a single arrival. That's the exponential distribution at work. Long gaps are not unusual when the rate is low.

## Connection to Theory

Here's the bridge back to the [previous post](/statistics/poisson-process). Fix any time $t$ and ask: what is $N(t)$? It's a random variable. Each run of the simulation, you get a different value. But the distribution of that random variable is exactly $\text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$.

The PMF we derived — $P(N(t) = k) = \frac{(\lambda t)^k e^{-\lambda t}}{k!}$ — tells you the probability of landing on each staircase height at time $t$. The simulation makes that abstract distribution concrete. When you watch the staircase and notice it usually ends somewhere close to $\lambda t = 24$ (for $\lambda = 3$, $t = 8$), and occasionally ends at 18 or 30, you're seeing the Poisson distribution play out in real time.
